**Date:** March 2026

## Abstract

This paper proposes a structural-functional framework for defining intelligence at a macro level. Rather than viewing intelligence as a measure of performance, task success, or domain-specific capability, the framework characterizes intelligence as a property emerging from the interaction of information, structure, weighting, and constraint.

The central claim is that intelligence is not determined solely by the quantity of information contained within a system, but by the system's capacity to traverse meaningful pathways through a structured and weighted informational space.

The framework is represented by:

**I = C × Φ(S, St, W)**

where intelligence emerges from the constrained space of traversable inference paths generated within a structured information system.

The model is intentionally substrate-independent and is intended as a general framework applicable to biological, artificial, computational, and hypothetical systems. Its purpose is not to measure intelligence directly, but to identify the structural conditions under which intelligent behavior becomes possible.

---

## 1. Introduction

Defining intelligence has long been one of the central challenges in philosophy, cognitive science, and artificial intelligence.

Many existing definitions focus on observable performance, problem-solving ability, learning efficiency, or goal-directed behavior. While these approaches are useful for evaluation, they often describe the manifestations of intelligence rather than the underlying conditions that make intelligent behavior possible.

This paper adopts a different perspective.

Instead of defining intelligence through outcomes, it seeks to define intelligence through structure.

The objective is not to explain how a particular intelligent system functions, but to identify the structural properties shared by all systems capable of producing intelligent behavior.

Under this view, intelligence is treated as a structural-functional property of an information system rather than a collection of specific abilities.

---

### 1.1 Scope and Generality

The framework is intentionally independent of any particular physical substrate.

It does not assume:
- biological neurons
- digital computers
- specific learning algorithms
- particular physical laws

Instead, it operates at a more abstract level, focusing only on:
- information
- structure
- weighting
- constraint

Because these concepts are substrate-independent, the framework may be applied to biological cognition, artificial intelligence systems, abstract computational systems, and other hypothetical forms of intelligence.

The aim is therefore not to describe intelligence in one particular world, but to identify structural principles that may be relevant wherever intelligence exists.

---

## 2. Core Definition of Intelligence

At the macro level, intelligence is defined as:

> The constrained capacity of a system to traverse a structured and weighted network of stored information in order to generate stable, coherent, and context-consistent inferences, adaptations, and novel combinations.

This definition shifts attention away from outputs and toward the underlying capacity that enables those outputs.

Intelligence is therefore viewed as a property of navigability within a structured informational system.

---

### 2.1 Functional Formulation

The framework is expressed as:

**I = C × Φ(S, St, W)**

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **I** | Intelligence (operational capacity) |
| **S** | Stored Information |
| **St** | Structure |
| **W** | Weights |
| **C** | Constraints |
| **Φ(S, St, W)** | The induced space of traversable inference pathways within the structured system |

The equation should not be interpreted as a finalized mathematical model. Rather, it serves as a structural representation of the components required for intelligent operation. The multiplication symbol is used as a compositional metaphor, not a strict algebraic operation.

---

### 2.2 Interpretation of Φ

Φ represents the operational space generated by information, structure, and weighting.

It does not refer to a single path, conclusion, or output. Instead, it represents the collection of inference pathways that become available within a structured information system.

Conceptually, Φ includes:
- generation of possible inference paths
- traversal of connected information
- prioritization of pathways through weighting
- selection of context-relevant routes

Thus, Φ captures not merely what information exists within a system, but what can be done with that information.

The intelligence of a system depends not only on the amount of information it contains, but also on the richness and accessibility of its traversable inference space.

---

## 3. Structural Representation

### 3.1 Information Graph Model

A system may be represented as a graph:

- **Nodes** represent informational elements
- **Edges** represent structural relationships
- **Edge weights** represent relational strength, significance, or accessibility

Within this representation:
- **S** corresponds to informational content
- **St** corresponds to graph structure
- **W** corresponds to weighted relationships

The graph serves as an abstract model of the informational organization of the system.

---

### 3.2 Traversal as Cognitive Operation

Within the framework, cognitive processes can be understood as forms of traversal.

Examples include:
- **Reasoning** as navigation through weighted inference paths
- **Creativity** as the formation of new pathways between distant regions of the graph
- **Problem-solving** as constraint-guided traversal toward coherent solutions
- **Generalization** as movement across structurally related regions

Different cognitive functions may therefore be interpreted as variations of the same underlying structural process.

---

### 3.3 Constraints as Stability Mechanisms

Constraints regulate traversal.

Without constraints, an information system may generate arbitrary or contradictory pathways.

Constraints contribute to intelligence by:
- limiting incoherent traversals
- preserving consistency
- stabilizing inference
- maintaining structural integrity

In this framework, constraints do not merely restrict behavior; they help create conditions under which meaningful traversal becomes possible.

---

## 4. Intelligence as Traversable Capacity

The framework proposes that intelligence is fundamentally linked to the capacity of a system to access and traverse meaningful pathways within its informational structure.

This view has several implications:

- A system containing large amounts of disconnected information may possess limited intelligence.
- Conversely, a system with fewer informational elements but highly organized and traversable pathways may exhibit greater intelligence.

Under this perspective, intelligence depends on:
- richness of informational structure
- effectiveness of weighting
- coherence of traversal
- stability provided by constraints

The emphasis shifts from informational quantity to informational usability.

---

## 5. Conceptual Advantages

### 5.1 Separation of Ontology and Measurement

The framework distinguishes between what intelligence is and how intelligence is measured.

Performance metrics may evaluate intelligence, but they do not necessarily define it.

The proposed framework focuses on the structural conditions that make intelligent performance possible.

---

### 5.2 Substrate Independence

Because the framework relies only on information, structure, weighting, and constraint, it is not tied to any specific implementation.

This allows the framework to be applied across diverse forms of intelligent systems.

---

### 5.3 Unified Interpretation of Cognitive Functions

Reasoning, creativity, learning, adaptation, and problem-solving can all be interpreted as different manifestations of structured traversal.

The framework therefore offers a common structural language for discussing diverse cognitive phenomena.

---

## 6. Limitations and Open Questions

The framework remains conceptual and incomplete in several respects.

### 6.1 Formal Definition of Φ

**A note on circularity:**  
One might observe that Φ is defined via "traversal" and "inference pathways," and these concepts themselves seem to presuppose some notion of intelligence or rules of reasoning. This is intentional. The framework does not attempt to define Φ from first principles without remainder. Rather, it specifies a relational constraint: any viable definition of Φ must be such that I = C × Φ(S, St, W) yields meaningful results. Finding a non-circular, computable definition of Φ is precisely the open problem stated here—not a hidden flaw of the framework.

The most significant open problem concerns the formalization of Φ.

Future work may explore graph-theoretic, probabilistic, or algorithmic approaches for defining and measuring traversable inference spaces.

---

### 6.2 Dynamic Evolution

The framework currently does not specify how structure and weights evolve over time.

Learning, adaptation, and memory formation require additional mechanisms that remain outside the scope of the present formulation.

---

### 6.3 Computational Complexity

As informational structures grow, the number of possible pathways may increase dramatically.

Understanding how intelligent systems efficiently navigate large traversal spaces remains an important challenge.

---

## 7. Conclusion

This paper proposes a structural-functional framework for understanding intelligence as a property of constrained informational traversal.

Rather than defining intelligence through behavior, performance, or specific implementations, the framework defines intelligence through the capacity of a system to navigate a structured and weighted informational space under stabilizing constraints.

The proposed formulation:

**I = C × Φ(S, St, W)**

provides a conceptual foundation for describing intelligence at a level independent of particular substrates, domains, or environments.

While substantial formalization remains necessary, the framework offers a unified perspective from which diverse forms of intelligence may be examined through a common structural lens.

---

## Final Statement

> Intelligence is not defined by what a system contains, but by what it can reliably access, connect, and generate within its structure.

The purpose of this framework is not to describe a particular intelligent system, but to identify the structural conditions that make intelligence possible.
